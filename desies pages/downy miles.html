Downy Mildew

Introduction

Downy mildew is a serious fungal-like disease that affects many plants, especially leafy crops. It appears as yellow or pale green patches on the leaves, often with grayish, downy growth on the undersides. Left untreated, it can significantly reduce plant growth and crop yield.

Causes

The disease is caused by oomycete pathogens and spreads rapidly under wet, humid, and poorly ventilated conditions. Overhead watering, dense planting, and high humidity favor the growth and spread of downy mildew.

Symptoms

  • Yellow or pale green patches on the upper leaf surface
  • Gray or white downy fungal growth on the undersides of leaves
  • Leaf curling, stunted growth, and wilting in severe cases
  • Premature leaf drop leading to reduced yield

Prevention

  • Plant disease-resistant varieties if available
  • Ensure good air circulation through proper spacing and pruning
  • Avoid overhead watering; prefer drip irrigation
  • Remove and destroy infected plant debris
  • Apply preventive fungicides when environmental conditions favor the disease

Treatment

  • Use recommended fungicides such as copper-based sprays or systemic fungicides
  • Remove severely infected leaves to reduce pathogen load
  • Improve drainage and reduce leaf wetness to prevent further spread

Fertilizers and Nutrition

Proper plant nutrition enhances resistance against downy mildew. Balanced application of NPK and micronutrients is recommended for healthy growth.

Nutrient Purpose Application Tips
Nitrogen (N) Promotes leaf growth Apply moderately; excess nitrogen can increase susceptibility
Phosphorus (P) Supports root development and overall plant resistance Follow soil test recommendations
Potassium (K) Strengthens plant tissues and reduces disease severity Apply regularly to maintain optimum levels
Micronutrients (Zn, Mn, Mg) Enhance immunity and stress tolerance Use foliar sprays or soil application as recommended

Additional Tips

  • Monitor plants regularly, especially in humid weather
  • Practice crop rotation to reduce pathogen build-up in soil
  • Maintain clean tools and equipment to prevent disease spread
  • Remove infected plants promptly to protect healthy crops
